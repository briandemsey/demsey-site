Peter and The Wolf: A Silicon Valley Fairy Tale

A symphonic satire for orchestra and narrator

Brian Demsey | 2025

← Back to Articles

A Symphonic Satire for Orchestra and Narrator The Characters and Their Instruments Peter - The Strings Silicon Valley Counterpart: Elon Musk The brash protagonist who ignores warnings from elders, ventures boldly where he's told not to go, and somehow captures the wolf through sheer audacity and unconventional tactics. Listen to how confident he sounds, how certain he is that he knows better than everyone else. The strings capture his grandiosity perfectly, for Peter believes he contains multitudes. He climbs trees, makes ropes out of nothing, wins anyway. The Bird - The Flute Silicon Valley Counterpart: Marc Andreessen Quick, fluttering, always tweeting. Notice how the flute never quite lands anywhere for long. The Bird circles around offering advice and assistance, helps the protagonist execute the plan. Light and nimble, moves fast. He chirps opinions about everything from software to civilization. The Duck - The Oboe Silicon Valley Counterpart: Adam Neumann This quacking sound represents a charismatic waterfowl who convinced everyone his puddle was actually an ocean. He waddles with tremendous confidence into the meadow, doesn't see the danger coming, gets swallowed whole by the wolf-but somehow survives inside, still quacking, and may yet emerge for a sequel. The oboe captures his honking pronouncements about "community" and "energy." The Cat - The Clarinet Silicon Valley Counterpart: Tim Cook The slinky clarinet moves carefully, deliberately. Stealthy, calculating, moves in careful staccato. The Cat watches from a safe distance, serves his own interests, never quite commits to helping but doesn't get eaten either. He sells you the same mouse three years in a row at premium prices, and somehow you're grateful. Grandfather - The Bassoon Silicon Valley Counterpart: Warren Buffett This grumpy sound is the voice of caution warning about dangers in the meadow. "Don't go out there with your crypto and your SPACs!" The bassoon is perfect for finger-wagging. Ignored by Peter, vindicated by events, still grumbling at the end. He has been warning everyone about the meadow for decades. "Diversify," he grumbles. "Avoid speculation." Nobody listens. The Wolf - The French Horns Silicon Valley Counterpart: Regulatory Bodies (FTC, SEC, EU) These menacing French horns represent not a single creature but a pack-the Regulators. They prowl at the edge of the forest, lurking, menacing. When they hunt, they hunt slowly, expensively, and with extensive documentation. The Wolf swallows the occasional duck, but ultimately gets captured and paraded to the zoo by the protagonist. The Hunters - Timpani and Bass Drum Silicon Valley Counterpart: Congress The kettle drums and bass drum represent the Hunters' gunshots-loud, late, and largely ineffective, but very enthusiastic about making noise. They arrive very late, want to shoot at something, but Peter has already solved the problem. They take credit anyway. The Blacksmith - The Glockenspiel Silicon Valley Counterpart: Jensen Huang This sparkling sound is like light glinting off silicon wafers. The Blacksmith is a quiet man in a leather jacket who forges tools in his workshop at the edge of the meadow. He does not chase wolves or climb trees. He does not argue with ducks about valuation. He simply makes the tools that everyone else needs, sells them to all sides, and collects his payment in cash. Notice that he is the only character whose instrument sounds like money being counted. He is the arms dealer of the meadow-inevitable. The Story [The conductor raises the baton. The narrator steps forward.] NARRATOR: Good evening, and welcome to our symphonic fairy tale. You have now met our characters. Let us begin. Part I: The Meadow [The strings play Peter's theme] Early one morning, Elon opened the gate of his Palo Alto compound and walked out into the great green meadow of the technology industry. The year was 2021, and money grew on trees. Interest rates were zero. SPACs bloomed like wildflowers. [The flute joins in] Perched on a high branch was his friend Marc the Bird. "Good morning!" chirped the Bird. "I have just posted a thread explaining why this meadow is actually the most important meadow in history, and why anyone who criticizes meadows is a Luddite." "Good morning," said Elon. "I have decided to buy the pond." [The oboe introduces the Duck's waddling theme] Swimming in the pond was Adam the Duck. "What kind of pond is this?" asked the Bird, fluttering down. "It's not a pond," said the Duck. "It's a community-based aquatic ecosystem. We don't sell water here. We sell belonging. We sell consciousness elevation. Our valuation is forty-seven billion dollars." "But I see water," said the Bird. "You're not thinking big enough," quacked the Duck. [The flute and oboe begin arguing musically-high chirps against nasal quacks] The Bird and the Duck began to argue. "What kind of bird are you," said the Duck, "if you cannot swim?" "What kind of duck are you," replied the Bird, "if you cannot fly-or turn a profit?" [The clarinet creeps in with the Cat's theme] While they argued, the Cat crept through the grass on velvet paws. "Perhaps," thought Tim the Cat, "while these fools bicker, I will introduce a new pond subscription service at $9.99 per month. And next year I will remove the dock and sell it separately." The Cat crept closer... closer... but the Bird saw him and flew up into the tree. The Duck quacked indignantly from the middle of the pond: "Our community does not support predatory behavior unless it's disruption!" The Cat sat beneath the tree and thought: "Is it worth climbing up so high? By the time I get there, the Bird will have flown away... and besides, my margins are already excellent." Part II: Grandfather's Warning [The bassoon intones gravely] Just then, Grandfather Warren came out. He was angry because Elon had gone into the meadow, which Grandfather considered speculative and overvalued. "This meadow is dangerous!" said Grandfather. "What if a wolf comes? You should be in index funds. You should be in Coca-Cola." "Grandfather," said Elon, "wolves are a legacy institution. I am not afraid of wolves. Also, I am purchasing Twitter." "What is Twitter?" grumbled Grandfather. "It's the Bird's pond," said Elon. "I will make it better by making it worse, then better again. It's very simple." [The bassoon plays a phrase of deep concern] Grandfather shook his head, took Elon by the arm, led him home, and locked the gate. Elon immediately climbed over the wall. Part III: The Wolf [The French horns announce the Wolf's theme-ominous, bureaucratic, inevitable] No sooner had Grandfather gone than a great gray Wolf came out of the forest. It had many heads: one marked FTC, another SEC, another EU COMPETITION AUTHORITY. Its fur was made of subpoenas. [The clarinet trembles; the Cat scrambles up the tree] In a twinkling, the Cat climbed up the tree. Tim knew when to disappear. "I have always complied with all applicable regulations," he said from a high branch. "I have the receipts." [The oboe quacks in panic] The Duck quacked in excitement. "Perhaps the Wolf wants to join our community! Our platform is very wolf-inclusive. We just need your social security number and-" [The oboe theme is cut short with a gulp from the French horns] The Wolf did not want to join the community. The Wolf lunged at the Duck and swallowed him in a single bureaucratic proceeding. [Silence. Then a muffled oboe quacking from inside the Wolf] Inside the Wolf's belly, the Duck continued to quack about his vision. "This is actually fine!" he honked from within. "I'm pivoting to digestive wellness! We're calling it WeGut!" Part IV: The Blacksmith's Workshop [The strings play Peter's theme-calculating, scheming] Elon watched from behind the wall. He saw the Wolf pacing below the tree, digesting the Duck and preparing additional enforcement actions. He had a plan-but he needed a tool. [The glockenspiel chimes its entrance] At the edge of the meadow stood the Blacksmith's workshop-a humble forge that glowed day and night with an almost supernatural heat. Inside, Jensen the Blacksmith worked his anvil, wearing the same leather jacket he had worn every day for thirty years. [The glockenspiel plays a steady, industrious rhythm-like chips being fabbed] Elon approached the workshop. Inside, he saw marvels: ropes that could think, nets that could learn, lassos woven from pure computational power. On the walls hung signs: "GEFORCE," "TESLA" (no relation), "H100," "BLACKWELL." "I need a rope," said Elon. "To catch the Wolf." [The glockenspiel pauses] The Blacksmith looked up from his work. He spoke rarely, but when he spoke, people listened-mostly at earnings calls. "A rope," said Jensen. "Yes. I have ropes. I have the finest ropes in the meadow. My ropes are in every tree, every trap, every plan to catch every wolf. My ropes power the Bird's tweets. My ropes train the Cat's algorithms. My ropes-" He paused, glancing toward the Wolf outside. "My ropes are also in the Wolf's digestive system. How do you think he processes all those ducks so efficiently?" [The French horns-the Wolf-growl in confirmation] "You sell to the Wolf?" asked Elon. "I sell to everyone," said the Blacksmith, returning to his anvil. "I am the arms dealer of the meadow. The Bird uses my chips to generate his threads. The Cat uses my chips to animate his emoji. Grandfather uses my chips to run his spreadsheets, though he complains about the electricity costs. Even the Duck-" [The oboe quacks mournfully from inside the Wolf] "-even the Duck had a data center full of my GPUs. It was mining something. I never asked what." [The glockenspiel resumes its shimmering rhythm] "I do not take sides," said Jensen. "I take market share. Now-do you want the rope or not? I should warn you: there is a waitlist. Demand is unprecedented. Everyone wants to catch wolves these days, or build wolves, or teach wolves to paint pictures and write poetry." "How long is the waitlist?" asked Elon. "Eighteen months. But for you-" the Blacksmith smiled slightly, "-six months, if you pay in advance and agree to publish the benchmarks." [The strings play an impatient theme] "I need it now," said Elon. "I'll pay triple." "Ah," said Jensen. "The magic words." [The glockenspiel plays a triumphant, transactional flourish] And from beneath his workbench, Jensen produced a rope unlike any other-woven from fiber-optic cable and infused with ten thousand tensor cores. It shimmered with latent intelligence. It could think faster than the Wolf could legislate. "This is the H100 Lasso," said the Blacksmith. "It will catch anything. It will hold anything. It is also excellent for training large language models, if you get bored of wolves." Elon took the rope. "What do I owe you?" Jensen told him. [A long pause. Even the strings seem to gasp.] "That's... a lot," said Elon. "Supply and demand," said the Blacksmith, returning to his anvil. "The meadow runs on my chips. The wolves run on my chips. The hunters will someday run on my chips, once Congress figures out what chips are. I am essential infrastructure. I am the shovel-seller in the gold rush. I am-" He smiled beneath his leather jacket. "-inevitable." [The glockenspiel plays a final, self-satisfied chime] Elon paid. He always paid. Everyone always paid. And the Blacksmith went back to forging the future, one shimmering chip at a time. Part IV-B: The Capture [The strings play Peter's theme, now daring and bold] Armed with the H100 Lasso, Elon returned to the meadow. "Bird," he said, "can you fly down and circle around the Wolf's head? Distract him with Web3 discourse?" "I can," said the Bird. "I will tweet about how the Wolf is threatened by innovation. I will compare him to the medieval church." [The flute plays a dizzying dance around the Wolf's theme] And so the Bird flew down and began circling the Wolf's head, daringly, almost touching the Wolf's jaws. Oh, how the Wolf snapped at him! How the Wolf wanted to silence him! But the Bird was too agile, and the Wolf could not read a fifty-tweet thread anyway. [The strings play a cunning motif] Meanwhile, Elon climbed the tree-past the Cat, who pretended not to notice-and lowered the rope slowly, slowly... [Tension builds in the orchestra] ...and caught the Wolf by the tail! [Brass fortissimo! Chaos!] The Wolf thrashed and howled, but the more he struggled, the tighter Elon pulled the rope. Part V: The Trial in the Meadow (The Apologia) [The French horns swell triumphantly-the Wolf's theme inverted into a judicial fanfare] NARRATOR: But wait. Our story takes a turn. For you see, the Wolf was not merely a predator. The Wolf was an institution. And institutions do not simply get caught by the tail. They convene hearings. [The strings fall silent. A single horn intones gravely.] Before Elon could drag the Wolf to the zoo, the Wolf stood upright and spoke: "You are charged," said the Wolf, "with corrupting the youth of the meadow. You are charged with impiety-with failing to believe in the established gods of this forest: Procedure, Compliance, and Antitrust Review. How do you plead?" [Silence. Then the strings begin Peter's theme-but slower, more contemplative] And here, dear listeners, we must tell you of an ancient story. Two thousand four hundred years ago, in the city of Athens, a man named Socrates stood before a jury of five hundred citizens. He too was charged with corrupting the youth. He too was charged with impiety-with not believing in the gods of his city, and introducing new gods of his own. Socrates did not apologize. He did not beg for mercy. Instead, he gave what the Greeks called an apologia-not an apology, but a defense. A defiant explanation of why he had lived as he lived, and why he would not change. He was convicted anyway, and sentenced to death. [The strings play a questioning phrase] Now Elon stood in the meadow, the Wolf before him, and he too chose to speak. [The strings begin softly, then build as Elon speaks] NARRATOR (as Elon): "Citizens of the meadow-or rather, Wolves of the regulatory forest-I stand before you accused of disruption. Of moving fast and breaking things. Of corrupting the young with dreams of electric cars and rockets and tunnels and neural interfaces and social media platforms purchased on impulse. [The flute-the Bird-plays an approving trill] "You say I do not believe in your gods. This is true. I do not worship at the altar of Quarterly Guidance. I do not bow before the shrine of Regulatory Capture. I do not burn incense to the sacred god of How Things Have Always Been Done. [The French horns growl] "You accuse me of introducing new gods. Also true. I have introduced the gods of First Principles Thinking and of Building the Future and of Posting Through It. These gods are strange to you. They do not file environmental impact statements. [The bassoon-Grandfather-grumbles a warning] "My grandfather here-and all the grandfathers of the meadow-say I am reckless. They say I should diversify. They say I should stay behind the gate. But I ask you: what is the unexamined meadow worth? A meadow where no one climbs the wall? A meadow where the ducks are safe but never learn to fly? [The oboe quacks mournfully from inside the Wolf's belly] "The duck believed in something. Foolishly, perhaps. His valuation was... creative. But he believed. And what do you believe in, Wolf? Process? Paperwork? The sacred right to review everything until nothing gets built? [The strings crescendo] "So I say to you what Socrates said to Athens: I am a gadfly. I sting the great horse of this meadow to keep it from sleeping. You may swat me. You may fine me. You may investigate me until I am buried in depositions. But I will not stop building. I will not stop posting. I will not apologize for dreaming larger than your forms allow. [A dramatic pause. The orchestra is silent.] "If the penalty for this is death-or delisting-so be it. I have lived as I chose to live. And I have excellent lawyers." [The French horns confer in low, bureaucratic murmurs] NARRATOR: The Wolf deliberated. [A long, grinding passage in the brass-the sound of committee meetings] "And what of the Blacksmith?" growled the Wolf. "He armed you for this insurrection!" [The glockenspiel plays an innocent trill] NARRATOR (as Jensen): "I am but a humble toolmaker. I forge chips. What others do with those chips is not my concern. I have sold equally to all parties. I am neutral. I am Switzerland, if Switzerland had a market cap of three trillion dollars." [The French horns grumble but cannot argue-they too run on his chips] After much discussion, the Wolf delivered its verdict: "We find you guilty of disruption in the first degree. We sentence you to... ongoing compliance monitoring, quarterly reporting requirements, and a consent decree that neither admits nor denies wrongdoing." [The strings play a defiant, almost mocking phrase] "However," the Wolf continued, "enforcement will be delayed pending appeal, budgetary review, and a change in administration." And so Elon was not sentenced to drink hemlock. He was sentenced to something far worse: years of litigation. But he had already climbed back over the wall, incorporated in Delaware, and moved his headquarters to Texas. Part VI: The Hunters [Timpani and bass drum: BOOM BOOM BOOM] Just then, the Hunters came out of the forest-Congress, following the Wolf's trail, firing shots into the air. "We're here to help!" shouted the Hunters. "We've formed a bipartisan subcommittee! We have concerns about monopolistic wolves! Also, could someone explain what a blockchain is?" [More booms, hitting nothing] "Don't shoot!" cried Elon from the tree. "The Wolf is already captured. Help me take him to the zoo. We'll call it regulatory clarity." "An excellent idea," said the Hunters. "We will hold hearings about this for several years." Part VII: The Triumphant Procession [The full orchestra plays a grand, satirical march] And now, imagine the triumphant procession. Elon marched at the front, holding the H100 Lasso, posting about his victory. Behind him, the Hunters carried the Wolf, arguing about jurisdiction. The Cat followed at a safe distance, announcing a new Wolf-tracking feature for iPhone-powered, naturally, by Jensen's chips. [The glockenspiel joins the march-shimmering above it all] And off to the side, walking alone, came Jensen the Blacksmith, still in his leather jacket. He had sold the rope to Elon. He had sold the processing power to the Wolf. He had sold chips to the Hunters for their "AI-enabled oversight initiative." He had even sold a small cluster to the Duck, whose data center continued to hum inside the Wolf's belly, mining cryptocurrency to pay off creditors. Jensen did not march with the victors or the vanquished. He simply collected from everyone and went home to build the next generation of tools. "The meadow will need faster ropes soon," he mused. "Faster wolves. Faster everything. And I will be ready." [The glockenspiel plays a theme that sounds almost like a cash register] The Bird flew overhead, explaining to his followers why he had been right about everything all along. Grandfather Warren brought up the rear, grumbling into his bassoon that the whole expedition had been overvalued and that nobody should have been in the meadow in the first place. [The muffled oboe quacks from inside the Wolf] And if you listened very carefully, you could hear the Duck, still quacking inside the Wolf's belly. "This is temporary!" quacked Adam. "We're restructuring! The fundamentals are strong! Our consciousness is still elevated!" [The march crescendos and ends with a flourish] Coda: The Allegory [After the final chord, the narrator speaks once more] NARRATOR: Socrates, you may recall, was offered the chance to escape his death sentence. His friends arranged it. He refused. He believed that accepting the laws of Athens-even unjust ones-was part of the philosophical life he had chosen. Elon, naturally, took a different lesson from the story. He bought the prison. [The oboe quacks one last time. Blackout.] Epilogue NARRATOR: And so our tale concludes. The Wolf was placed in a regulatory zoo, where he continues to process paperwork. The Bird still tweets. The Cat still profits. Grandfather still grumbles. The Blacksmith still forges, selling to all sides, counting his shimmering coins. And Elon? Elon is already planning his next expedition-this time to the Moon, or perhaps to buy the forest itself. As for the Duck... some say he's still in there, pivoting. [The orchestra plays a final chord. The glockenspiel shimmers. The oboe quacks one last time.] THE END by Brian Demsey brian@hallucinations.cloud
